Tinkercad

Get Started

Tinkercad is a web browser-based entry-level CAD program from Autodesk, Inc.. Tinkercad uses a simplified constructive solid geometry method of constructing models. A design is made up of primitive shapes that are either "solid" or "hole". Combining solids and holes together, new shapes can be created, which in turn can be assigned the property of solid or hole.

Below is the most basic project modeling interface of Tinkercad, we can see that there are some built-in shapes in the sidebar.

Tinkercad offers an entire shapes library as shown below. In addition to the standard library of primitive shapes, a user can create custom shape generators using a built-in JavaScript editor, but since I am new to CAD, I will not touch that part.

I first wanted to try to build solid objects. I created a text object and typed "LOL". I then added the roof and pyramid to the top and a side. Now the objects are separate:

Drag the checkbox to select all the objects and click on the "Group" tool in the upper right corner, and we have combined these models into one!

We can also change the object's property to hole, then the object can cut the overlapping part from another solid object

Hole objects can also be grouped.

Together with importing and exporting model files, that's pretty much all the operations Tinkercad can do. It's really a CAD with only the most basic functionality, but because of that it can run on browser and is very beginner friendly.

First Remix

After familiarizing myself with Tinkercad's capabilities, I decided to find a simple model to remix for practice. I found this pull handle model on thangs.com:

Remixing is not supposed to be limited to the original nature and use of the model. In connection with those shapes built into Tinkercad, I let myself brainstorm and conceptualize the following sketch for remixing:

This basically consists of a solid pull handle, a solid cylinder, and a hole cylinder. My main idea was to cut a thinner cylinder with no through ground in the cylinder and place the reduced pull handle vertically on the side of the cylinder to form a mug-like cup.

Since Tinkercad does not support the input dimension parameter, I will only be concerned with the relative position of objects in this design.

The image below shows the basic components I will need to build my design. The pull handle is imported and rotated to align the grid lines. Cylinders are built-in shapes. To keep both cylinders on the same axis, I cloned the drawed solid cylinder to the same position. Then, I change the new one to hole and scale it down. The image below separates the two columns in order to disguise them.

By placing the three objects in the correct relative positions according to the sketch and grouping them, a simple model remix is complete!

After exporting the project as a .stl model file and importing it into PrusaSlicer, I realized that the default dimensions of the model were larger than I expected. Despite the simplicity of the structure, this was my first remix project after all, and to be able to observe the difference between modeling and actual printing as quickly as possible, I scaled down the actual print size to one-eighth (one-half in each dimension) of the original size.

Based on my knowledge of the printer's capabilities in Project 1, the overhang portion of the pull handle does not require support. With the print completed, my prediction proved to be correct.

Of course, these bothersome adhesive filament issues are also made more apparent by the smaller spacing.

After confirming that the finished print matched my design, I felt comfortable reprinting the model at the default size. Just in case, I used the organic support to prevent overhang problems with the pull handle printing.

Thus, my first CAD remixing project was completed with satisfactory printing results.

Fusion 360

Also a CAD software from Autodesk Inc., Fusion 360 is more professional, but that means more built-in tools and more freedom to create.

After looking at the online modules, for such a software, I would like to try to design a remix project that focuses on my use of the software tools.

The first thing that popped into my head was the Apple Inc. logo. Instead of using 3D models as the basis for remixing, I think it would be an interesting experiment to use 2D designs as the basis for remixing, after all, extrusion from 2D sketches is a very important and useful tool of Fusion 360.

Below is a third-party reverse parse of the Apple Inc. logo that I found online.

I thought some of the parsing parts were a bit redundant, so I referred to its parsing ideas, redesigned the logo, and drew a simplified sketch in Fusion 360:

All values are labeled in the figure in millimeters. I used creation tools like line, circle, and sketch dimension,as well as constraints tools like tangent, equal, bisect, and fix in my sketching process.

At this point, I'm starting to wrestle with how to design a 3D object based on this logo. My girlfriend inspired me at this point: to design a flip box.

After understanding what each tool does, realizing this design is not complicated. So the next section will only briefly describe the important steps.

Rotate to create the apple body.

Rotate to cut out the bite.

Rotate to create the leaf.

Split the body into 2 parts using the offset plane.

Rotate to create connection between upper body and leaf.

Shell both parts of the body to create storage space.

Fillet the curves of the bite. (It actually took a while for me to decide which edges to fillet.)

Extrude from sketch and surface to create upper and lower joints.

Extrude from sketch to create spindle.

Extrude to create signature.

Fillet minor edges to decorate.

So far, my design has been successfully modeled. Notice that my experience with the last project showed me that the Prusa Mini + printer has a tolerance of around 0.20 mm, so I allowed 0.20 mm when modeling the spindle and joints.

I exported each of the three sections as .stl files and imported them into PrusaSlicer.Due to time constraints, I set the print settings down for each:

Bottom body: 0.20mm SPEED, Organic Support on build plate only, 10% infill

Top body: 0.20mm SPEED, Snug Support everywhere, 10% infill

Spindle: 0.20mm SPEED, with Brim, 50% infill

To make sure I caught the tolerance issues early, I printed the joint cut from the bottom body with the spindle for testing.

The spindle printed quickly and passed the tolerance test perfectly.

The top and bottom bodies of the box were printed then at the same time. (I was pleasantly surprised that the colors in the bottom part picked up right where the top part left off, what a coincidence!)

Just as I was happily assembling the box, the leaf connection broke.

That's when I saw that there was no infill in the middle of the connector. I rechecked the PrusaSlicer slicing results and realized I had ignored this issue.

Also, I wasn't able to close my box tightly after assembly.

I just realized that while I have tolerances for the spindle, I didn't leave space for the upper and lower body contact surfaces.

Overall, I am still very immature in this area of designing with CAD, but I consider this to be successful and educational as my early design projects.

Reflections

Throughout this entire project, my previous printing experience allowed me to avoid quite a few problems, but at the same time, I made quite a few mistakes and missed quite a few concepts while approaching new territory.

First of all, when designing a model, it is important to take into account as much space as possible for the contacting parts of everything. Of course, this comes with experience, for example, after this, I will try both mitering and whole layer removal as solutions to tolerances.

Secondly, how to arrange the printing direction is also worth studying. This can affect both the appearance and feel of the object, as well as the physical strength of the object. For example, my box gets rough when printed to a gently curved surface, and I may want to refer to this video on how to improve curved surfaces in future projects. As well as my vertically printed spindle cannot be utilized in higher torque scenarios.

Also, although this time it was due to time constraints, I think I'll need to be patient with this whole 3D printing thing in the future. This includes printing speed, infill, support and all these aspects, and not sacrificing the quality of the print for efficiency.

All in all, this project has once again enhanced my skills and pointed out my shortcomings, which I hope I will keep in mind when designing in the future.
